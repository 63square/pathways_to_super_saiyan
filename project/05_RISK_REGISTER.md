# 05 — Risk Register

## Methodology

Each risk is scored on:
- **Probability** (1–5): 1 = <5%, 2 = 5–20%, 3 = 20–50%, 4 = 50–80%, 5 = >80%
- **Severity** (1–5): 1 = nuisance, 2 = minor program impact, 3 = phase delay, 4 = phase failure, 5 = program-ending or life-threatening
- **Risk Score** = P × S (1–25)

Risks scoring ≥12 require active mitigation plan with named owner. Risks ≥20 require Board-level monitoring.

## 5.3 Insurance Strategy

The captive (Bermuda-domiciled) carries:
- **Medical malpractice:** $50M aggregate (covers CMO and treating physicians for procedures on Principal)
- **D&O:** $25M aggregate (covers Board and C-suite)
- **General liability:** $20M
- **Cyber:** $30M
- **Errors & omissions:** $15M

Commercial reinsurance on top up to $200M aggregate for medical and D&O. Cost: ~$8M Year 1 captive premiums + commercial layer.

The Principal is **personally indemnified** by the holdco for all program-related claims, with a Cook Islands trust structure separately shielding personal assets.

## 5.4 Black-Swan Scenarios

Risks not in the register because they are too low-probability to score meaningfully, but with severity high enough to warrant a one-paragraph contingency:

**BS1 — Major terrorist or state action against the program.** Mitigation: physical security, low public profile, no co-location of Principal with key IP. Contingency: rapid relocation drill quarterly.

**BS2 — Pandemic disrupts global supply and travel.** Mitigation: dual-located inventory; remote-work-capable for 60%+ of roles; medical supply 6-month buffer at Próspera.

**BS3 — A program protocol triggers a regulatory firestorm globally (cf. He Jiankui).** Mitigation: no germline; clear pre-publication; staged disclosure strategy.

**BS4 — Principal becomes mentally incapacitated.** Mitigation: standing health-care directive specifies whether and how program continues; trust structure handles asset management independently.

**BS5 — A program staff member commits a serious crime, scandal attaches to program.** Mitigation: background checks; ethics training; named-individual indemnification limits.

**BS6 — AGI arrives and obsoletes the entire program (or makes it trivially easy).** Mitigation: this is the "good problem to have"; budget held for rapid pivot; relationships with Anthropic/OpenAI/Google DeepMind via SAB advisors.

## 5.5 The "Honest Failure Mode" Analysis

What is the most likely way this program fails? Ranked:

1. **Slow leak of motivation** (35%): Phase 0 partial success leads to Phase 1, Phase 1 is harder than expected, Phase 2 budget seems excessive given marginal returns. Program drifts into "expensive longevity hobby." **Mitigation:** Pre-committed phase gates, sunset clauses, external SAB pressure.
2. **Principal medical event** (15%): A non-program medical event (accident, unrelated diagnosis) ends or pauses participation. **Mitigation:** Insurance, succession planning for the institutional assets even without Principal as subject.
3. **A specific intervention causes real harm** (10%): Triggers Phase pause, regulatory attention, family/legal pressure. **Mitigation:** SOPs, shadow cohort, conservative dosing.
4. **Talent retention failure** (10%): Cannot keep top scientists for 5+ year horizons in a non-academic, non-major-pharma environment. **Mitigation:** Compensation, prestige (Nobel-track work), Próspera as differentiator.
5. **Adverse public attention** (10%): Activist or press campaign forces dissolution. **Mitigation:** Low profile, legal preparedness, jurisdictional shielding.
6. **All other modes combined** (20%).

The honest failure modes are mundane. The catastrophic failures are improbable. **The program is designed to survive most failure modes with IP/spinout value intact.**
